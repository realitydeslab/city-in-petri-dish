## Field: Title of the Proposed Work

City in a Petri Dish - Stigmergic Energy Cultures for a Living City

## Field: Short Description of the Work, Suitable for Publication, Up To 150 Words

City in a Petri Dish is an interactive city-energy simulation staged as a living
culture under glass. Projected into a petri dish on a table, San Francisco becomes a
colony of building-organisms that metabolise solar and ground energy, spend energy to
maintain their height, and leave visible traces for one another through an evolving
energy network. The visitor plays an elemental force: adding light, seeding
geothermal surplus, drawing clouds and storms, and changing weather conditions to see
the city reorganise in real time. Behind the spectacle is a policy and cooperation
simulation: buildings may compete alone, or share surplus through self-organised
districts that emerge from local connections. The work reimagines the city as a
living complex adaptive system, where energy use becomes stigmergy: each building's
state alters the environment that other buildings inherit.

## Field: Full Proposal, Up To 750 Words

City in a Petri Dish asks what a city would become if it had to stay alive by
earning, storing, and sharing energy. The work presents a browser-based simulation as
an artificial life specimen: a miniature San Francisco projected into a physical
petri dish on a table, where buildings behave less like inert infrastructure than
like organisms in culture. They feed on light and ground energy, spend energy to
maintain their built mass, wither under scarcity, and regrow when conditions improve.
The result is not a fixed urban model but a real-time living system: a city that
continually reorganises itself in response to environmental pressure.

The visitor does not simply operate a menu. They play an atmospheric and geological
force. A solar beam can be swept across the dish, causing districts to bloom under
the cursor and decay when the light leaves. A geothermal gesture can seed local
ground-energy surplus, stabilising neighbourhoods that would otherwise fail under
cloud or dusk. Weather tools let the visitor paint clouds, storms, overcast skies,
and wind into the scene. These interventions are deliberately simple, almost divine:
add sun, add heat, darken the sky, push the weather, and watch the city answer. What
emerges is complex. Towers rise where energy is abundant, collapse where energy is
scarce, and form shifting zones of growth, dependency, and abandonment.

The central artificial-life mechanism is energy stigmergy. In social insects,
stigmergy describes coordination through traces left in a shared environment. Here,
the trace is energetic rather than chemical: every building's generation, demand,
surplus, deficit, height, and survival state changes the conditions for its
neighbours. A tall district creates demand; a low sunlit district creates surplus; a
clouded district becomes a sink; a geothermal patch becomes a refuge. The city is
therefore not planned from above. It is continuously written by local energetic
consequences, then read by the next round of building behaviour.

Behind the visual growth is a policy and cooperation simulation. Each building is an
agent with a fitness ratio: generation divided by demand. When isolated, buildings
survive only on their own energy balance, so cloudy or dense areas quickly starve.
When cooperation is enabled, buildings form a spatial graph that partitions itself
into energy districts. Surplus is first pooled within each district, then transferred
between districts when one community can support another. The projected network makes
this legible: gold buildings generate surplus, cyan buildings survive as receivers,
and moving packets show energy flowing through the city. Toggling policy from
isolation to sharing changes the organism's fate. A city of individual competitors
fragments; a city with mutual aid maintains shadowed or high-demand districts longer.

This makes the work both an artwork and a playable argument about urban energy
transition. Climate, renewable generation, morphology, and governance are usually
discussed as separate layers: weather belongs to environment, buildings to design,
policy to institutions, energy to infrastructure. City in a Petri Dish folds them
into one observable artificial organism. A visitor can make a heat-rich but cloudy
city, a sun-rich but selfish city, or a storm-battered city whose survival depends on
cooperation. Each condition reveals a different urban metabolism.

The project is grounded in architectural energy research developed with Xinwei
Zhuang, including rapid urban solar assessment and hierarchical urban graphs for
decentralised energy autonomy. The artwork translates that research into an
experience suitable for ALIFE: not a static visualisation of a result, but a lifelike
system that adapts, reorganises, and exposes its own rules through interaction. It
connects directly to the conference theme, "Living and Lifelike Complex Adaptive
Systems", by treating the city as life-as-it-could-be: an artificial ecology of
buildings, weather, energy, policy, and cooperation.

For in-person presentation, the preferred format is a downward projection onto a
large petri dish or circular glass dish placed on a table. The browser artwork can
also run on a wall projection, monitor, or online link. It requires no network once
loaded, no biological material, and no hazardous equipment. The piece can loop
unattended, while visitors intervene by trackpad, mouse, touch surface, or performer
operation. In all formats, the experience remains the same: the audience perturbs a
small city-world and watches it behave like a living complex system.

## Field: Relevance to Artificial Life / Conference Theme

City in a Petri Dish directly addresses ALIFE 2026's theme, "Living and Lifelike
Complex Adaptive Systems", by treating the city as an artificial organism rather than
as a static planning diagram. Buildings are agents whose growth, survival, and
collapse are governed by local energy rules. The city self-organises through
stigmergic traces of energy generation, demand, surplus, deficit, and sharing.
Weather and renewable energy become environmental pressures; policy becomes an
adaptive cooperation rule; districts emerge from local graph structure rather than
from central planning. The piece engages artificial life through morphogenesis,
self-organisation, emergence, decentralised cooperation, and life-as-it-could-be in a
social and ecological urban system.

## Field: Accessibility / Audience Interaction

The work can be presented in several interaction modes: visitors can interact
directly through trackpad, mouse, or touchscreen; the artist can perform guided
demonstrations; or the simulation can run as a continuous projection for audiences
who prefer not to operate the controls. Visual contrast is strongest in dim lighting.
The piece is silent and does not require audio access.

## Field: Additional Notes / Anything Else The Jury Should Know

This is a low-risk installation technically: it runs in a browser, needs no network
after loading, and has no biological or hazardous materials. The main spatial request
is the preferred petri-dish projection setup. If table projection is not feasible,
the same work can be shown as a wall projection, monitor kiosk, online piece,
documentation screening, or short presentation.

The work connects a computational ALife artwork to active architectural research on
urban solar assessment and decentralised energy autonomy. Its central contribution is
to make urban energy policy perceptible as a living adaptive process: visitors can
perturb weather and energy conditions, change cooperation rules, and watch the city
self-organise in real time.
